SUMMARY: The deadliest mass shooting in Texas history has brought to light a number of moral and ethical issues. The shooter, Devin Kelley, was a former U.S. serviceman with a history of domestic violence and mental health issues who slipped through the cracks of the system. Kelley escaped from a mental hospital in 2012 and was later convicted of assaulting his first wife and stepson while serving in the Air Force. Despite this, he was still able to purchase guns and carry out the shooting in Sutherland Springs, Texas, killing 26 people, including an unborn child.

The Air Force failed to enter Kelley's conviction into a government database used for screening prospective gun buyers, which has been called an "appalling" lapse by Representative Mac Thornberry. This case has exposed a weak link in the system of background checks for gun purchases. Kelley's private life was also marked by turbulence, with a history of behavior problems and a dispute with his in-laws that may have been a motivating factor in the shooting.

The massacre has once again brought the issue of gun control to the forefront, with some calling for stricter background checks and laws to prevent domestic abusers from purchasing guns. However, President Trump believes stricter gun laws would have resulted in more deaths, not fewer. The moral implications of this tragedy are clear, and it remains to be seen what actions will be taken to prevent similar incidents in the future.
